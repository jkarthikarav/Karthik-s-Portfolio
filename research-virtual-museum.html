Research Design Case Study

To Revamp a Virtual Museum Website

This case study dives into research focused on virtual exhibition websites, analyzing trends, best practices, and user engagement strategies. I conducted competitor analysis, designed research plans, and derived insights through analysing research data to structure their offerings, engage their audiences and for future improvements.

Client Context

A digital cultural institution offering virtual exhibitions and interactive learning experiences sought to better understand its competitive landscape. With growing interest in online cultural content, the organization aimed to assess how similar platforms were engaging audiences, structuring offerings, and positioning their brands.

Objective

The organization aimed to refresh its website design to better serve as a central access point for its growing collection of learning resources and virtual exhibitions. While the site currently hosted only one exhibition, the goal was to lay the groundwork for future expansions and improve the overall user experience, engagement, and discoverability of its content.

Why: Allowed us to identify successful strategies and design features, and to pinpoint areas where the client could differentiate itself.

Competitor Analysis

The competitor analysis focused on three types of museum websites: virtual-only museums, physical museums with virtual components, and Hispanic/Latin cultural museums.
The analysis involved a detailed review of how these museums structured their websites and facilitated user access to their virtual experiences.

Core Insights from Competitor Analysis
Similar Website Layouts

Museums often use similar website layouts to provide access to and promote their virtual experiences. Common features include multiple access points to virtual exhibits and high-contrast CTAs for easy navigation.

Audience Targeting

School students and educational institutions are frequently targeted for virtual experiences, suggesting a strong educational component in digital museum strategies.

Multiple Access Points

Digital/virtual experiences are often made accessible through various links, pages, and sections, giving users flexibility in how they interact with content.

Clear CTAs

Prominent calls to action and action buttons guide users from initial exposure to fully engaging with the virtual experiences, ensuring a smooth and clear user journey.

Primary Research: Qualitative and Quantitative Methods

Target Audience

Culturally curious Canadians; 25 Survey Participants, 6 new users who had not interacted with the website before

Observational Interviews

Conducted via Zoom for a duration of 1 hour and consisted of two parts:Exploratory Observation, and Usability Taskson the website.

Survey Design

Surveys were conducted using Google Forms to gather quantitative data on user preferences, expectations, and experiences with virtual museum websites.

Consent

All participants were asked for informed consent and were made aware of their right to withdraw from the study at any time.

Core Insights from Primary Research
Lack of Intuitive Navigation

The current navigation menu is not user-friendly, and the information architecture is confusing, making it difficult for users to find relevant content.

Outdated Design Elements

The websiteâ€™s content is overshadowed by outdated design layouts and elements, affecting the overall user experience and engagement.

Unclear CTAs and Interactions

Users struggled to identify the available features and calls to action (CTAs) on the site, leading to confusion about how to interact with the virtual exhibits.

Information Overload

The site presents too much information at once, leading to distractions and difficulty in locating key content.

Design Suggestions

Based on our research insights, I suggested:

Restructuring the Site Map and Navigation Menu for clearer, more intuitive navigation.

Optimizing the Content Layout/Structure to ensure content is more accessible and well-organized.
