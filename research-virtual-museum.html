Project Goals

This project was part of a UX research sprint aimed at improving a virtual museum’s digital platform.
The client wanted to understand how users interacted with exhibitions and learning resources as well as how to serve both casual visitors and educators better.
Research should inform design.
I co-led the research, synthesized insights, and proposed UI changes.
This case study focuses on how we used evidence to shape interface decisions.
Key Research Questions
Clarify Site Navigation
Does the site help users intuitively find exhibitions, resources, and learning tools?
Benchmark with Peers
What can we learn from how other virtual museums engage their audiences?
Design Priorities
Plan for Growth
Can the design support future exhibitions and content expansion?
Ensure Accessibility
What changes improve usability for diverse audiences?

Discovery

To explore how users engage with virtual museum content, we applied a mixed-methods research approach, combining desk research, competitor analysis, surveys, and 1:1 Zoom interviews. This helped us ground the redesign in real user behavior and expectations.

Desk Research

I studied current UX trends in virtual museums, focusing on accessibility, content structure, and engagement. Reviewed published case studies and reports from cultural institutions and design firms.

Competitor Analysis

I analyzed navigation structures, interaction models, and exhibit entry flows from hybrid and fully virtual museums for strengths, friction points, and opportunities.

Surveys & Usability Interviews

All four team members collected data from survey participants. Team member (2) conducted usability interviews. I co-designed the questionnaire, created usability tasks, and helped lead thematic analysis of all data.

Insights Gained

Core Insights from Competitor Analysis and Primary Research led to targeted design improvements.
Core Insights from Competitor Analysis

Similar Website Layouts

They use similar layouts to provide access to and promote their virtual experiences.

Audience Targeting

Educational institutions are frequently targeted, suggesting a strong educational component in digital strategies.

Multiple Access Points

Digital/virtual experiences are made accessible through various links, pages, and sections, giving flexibility in how they interact.

Clear CTAs

Prominent calls to actions guide users from initial exposure to fully engaging with the virtual experiences.

Core Insights from Primary Research

Lack of Intuitive Navigation

The current navigation menu is not user-friendly, and IA is confusing, making it difficult to find relevant content.

Outdated Design Elements

The website has outdated design layouts and elements, affecting engagement.

Unclear CTAs and Interactions

Users struggled to identify the available features and calls to action (CTAs) on the site.

Information Overload

The site presents too much information, leading to difficulty locating key content.

Design Suggestions

Restructure Site Map & Navigation

Restructuring the Site Map and Navigation Menu for clearer, more intuitive navigation.

Optimize Content Layout

Optimizing the Content Layout/Structure to ensure content is more accessible and well-organized.

Establish Design System & Guidelines

Establishing Style Guidelines & Design System to create consistent design elements and a cohesive visual language that aligns with the museum’s brand.

Turning Research into Actionable Design

This section shows how user insights were translated into clear, purposeful interface decisions. Unfortunately due to the NDA, I cannot reveal what the original site looked like, but the UI designs I made here are based upon the insights obtained previously.
Redesigned Resource Hub

I suggested a shift from a submenu-based access model to a hub-style landing page for educational resources. This card-based hub solves for unclear CTAs, overwhelming menus, and scattered content pathways found in the original experience.

Solved Insights

Lack of Intuitive Navigation

All educational resources are now in one place — no more digging through menus.

Unclear CTAs and Interactions

Each resource card includes a clear ‘Read’ or ‘View’ action so users know what to do.

Information Overload

Short descriptions and visual tags help users scan and find content quickly.

Audience Targeting

Tag filters let educators quickly sort by themes like heritage, gaming, or statistics.

Reimagined Embassy & Consulate Access

I suggested a shift from a static flag-and-link layout to a more dynamic, dropdown-based model. A dropdown design simplifies navigation, reducing visual clutter while making it faster to find the correct embassy or consulate.

Solved Insights

Lack of Intuitive Navigation

Dropdown improves navigation by grouping country selections in one place, making it easier to find the relevant embassies and consulates.

Information Overload

By hiding unnecessary links under a dropdown, users can focus only on the information that matters to them.

Optimize Content Layout

The dropdown consolidates it into one organized area as only relevant embassy and consulate links appear after selecting a country.

Conclusion: Thoughts and Takeaways

This project demonstrates how user research can directly shape interface decisions for online cultural platforms. Although the final UI designs are covered by an NDA, the recommendations were grounded in data collected through competitor analysis, surveys, and user interviews.

We identified key issues in the original experience, including confusing navigation, scattered content, and unclear calls to action. Each insight led to specific, practical design suggestions that improved the site’s structure and clarity while supporting future growth.

Key Takeaway: Research-Driven Design

My approach centered on turning real user feedback into purposeful UI strategies. This case study shows how I use research to guide design decisions that make digital spaces more engaging, intuitive, and accessible for diverse audiences.
