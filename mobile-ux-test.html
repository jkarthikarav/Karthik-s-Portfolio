Phase 1: Discovery Research: Interviews

Objective

Understand real user needs and behaviors related to museum visits and ticket purchasing in Toronto.

Methodology

We went out and spoke to the following demographic.

Students

Newcomers

Families

Seniors

Validated Insights

Low Awareness of Deals:

Many museum-goers are unaware of existing discounts or free ticket opportunities. This is consistent with broader research showing that cultural event discounts are often poorly publicized, leading to underutilization by target audiences.

Perceived Effort vs. Benefit:

Users frequently feel that searching for deals is not worth the effort due to scattered or hard-to-find information. This matches findings from studies on digital ticketing, where users abandon searches if discounts are not clearly presented or easily accessible.

Trusted Information Sources:

Participants rely on a mix of word-of-mouth, official museum websites, and social media, but express skepticism about outdated or incomplete information on these channels.

Phase 2: Competitive Benchmarking & Secondary Research

Objective

Analyze ticketing flows and information presentation across major Toronto museums.

Key Findings

Inconsistent Digital Flows:

Toronto museums vary widely in how they present ticketing and discount information online. Some, like the Gardiner Museum, offer pay-what-you-wish admission and clear free entry times, while others require visitors to search for eligibility or download separate documents.

Fragmented Information:

There is no centralized calendar or platform that aggregates cultural events and ticket deals across Toronto museums. Users must visit multiple sites, increasing friction and discouraging exploration.

Financial Barriers:

Financial means remain a significant barrier for many Torontonians, particularly students, newcomers, and families with limited budgets. While some museums offer free or discounted admission, these opportunities are often not widely known or are perceived as difficult to access.

Meet Mary, the Museum Go-er!

Mary was a fictitious human birthed by the group's research data. A hard-working and busy student, when time allows, she opts to visit museums with her friends or even solo!

Mary’s attempt to visit a museum in the modern day and age mirrors the challenges faced by many Torontonians. Despite prolonged efforts to find discounted tickets, the benefits are overshadowed by the time spent searching.

The delay results in exceeding her budget, highlighting the difficulties in navigating the process of obtaining affordable tickets for museum visits.

Mary helped us develop the feature list and our user journey. Our priority feature matrix helped sort ideas by impact and feasibility, ensuring alignment with what the users truly needed.

Conclusion: Thoughts and Takeaways

The goal of this project was to create a clean, functional UI that simplifies how Torontonians discover and access museum ticket deals.

Designing around real-world fragmentation (many museums, different systems) required a UI that could bring coherence without overwhelming the user. Through visual hierarchy, smart filtering, and minimal navigation, we created a prototype that felt both local and user-friendly.

Also, I envisioned it as an AI-powered interface: its recommendations and event highlights would adapt based on user preferences and behaviour data, improving relevance over time.

Key Takeaway: Design clarity

When systems behind the scenes are messy, UI must work harder to maintain simplicity. The interface must earn the user’s trust by making complexity invisible.
