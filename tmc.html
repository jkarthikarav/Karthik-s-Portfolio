Project Goals

The museum wanted to modernize how visitors found information about events, exhibits, and memberships. While content existed, it was buried in inconsistent menus and vague page structures.
It wasn’t a lack of information.
The redesign became an opportunity to streamline wayfinding, reframe the information architecture, and help the museum welcome its audiences more effectively online.

Our aim was to propose a navigation system that was intuitive, clearly labeled, and aligned with the museum’s organizational goals.
Research Plan: How can we improve the discoverability of content and wayfinding across the website?

We conducted a comprehensive content inventory, followed by card sorting and tree testing to explore how users expected information to be grouped. These methods helped us define a new navigation structure aligned with user expectations and institutional priorities.

Design Process

The project wasn’t just about refining the layout or visuals; it was about reshaping the entire user experience by focusing on improving the information architecture (IA) and overall flow.
Step 1: UX Research
This step involved conducting a content audit and card sorting exercises to gather insights into the current IA. The research helped define a new navigation structure that better aligned with both user needs and the museum's goals.
Step 2: IA Refinement
After initial wireframing, we focused on creating high-fidelity mockups of the IA. This phase refined the navigation system to ensure ease of access to key sections like ticketing, events, and donations.
Step 3: Report & Presentation
Finally, we prepared a comprehensive report and presentation to communicate the research findings, IA design, and refined navigation system to stakeholders. This phase helped gain buy-in and approval for the final design.

Proposed Information Architecture

Our proposed IA was shaped by content audit findings and card sort insights. The new structure improves clarity, reduces redundancy, and enhances navigation. The final solution now reflects the following changes:
  • Consolidated and streamlined navigation categories based on card sort patterns
  • Reorganized content to reduce duplication and improve clarity
  • Introduced clearer labels for pages to enhance user understanding
  • Refined menu structure to support intuitive wayfinding
  • Aligned IA with user expectations and core museum priorities

Implementation Strategy

Phase 1: The short-term solution that will integrate the new consolidated information architecture into the existing navigation menu design. This will allow testing with real users and gathering metrics, while avoiding disruption from a complete overhaul of the navigation system. It will also address accessibility issues, as the primary and secondary colors lack sufficient contrast.​








Phase 2: The long-term solution that brings the menu out of the hamburger. This approach also allowed for more flexible use of color, to improve the design using the WCAG contrast and accessibility guidelines.











Conclusion: Thoughts and Takeaways

The goal of this project was to redesign the navigation for the Textile Museum of Canada, making it easier for users to find information without feeling lost.

The biggest challenge was organizing the content in a way that made sense for different types of visitors while keeping the design simple and functional. By restructuring the menu and improving labels, users can now access key sections like exhibitions, collections, and resources more easily.

Key Takeaway: Looking at How a Solution can be implemented

While a navigation menu redesign could theoretically work, the question of whether the redesigned menu or the IA is being tested comes to the fore. This is why I suggested two solutions: a short term to test the IA and to also familiarise audiences with it and then the Long term to test the design.
